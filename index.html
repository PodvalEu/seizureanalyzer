Medications don’t work instantly — some take days or weeks to kick in. This asks: “If we gave a drug today, do seizures drop 3 days later? 7 days? 14 days?” For each drug and each delay, we take the dose on a given day and compare it to the total number of seizures over a window starting at that delay. The window size is different for each drug, matched to how long it takes to reach steady state (e.g. 5 days for Orfiril, 21 days for Fycompa). The strength of that link is measured as a score r (ranging from −1 to +1). Negative r (green) = when the dose goes up, seizures tend to go down — the drug seems to help. Positive r (red) = seizures tend to go up with the dose — no benefit seen (or seizures were already rising when the dose was increased). The delay with the strongest green value tells you roughly how long that drug takes to show its effect. This is a statistical pattern, not proof of cause and effect.

What is this? The line goes up on bad days and down on good days. Where it drifts far enough to matter, a dot appears: green ▼ = things got better, red ▲ = things got worse. This finds when seizures actually shifted — which may or may not line up with a drug change. Dashed vertical lines show drug changes for comparison.

Reading the table: “Magnitude” = how big the shift was. “Nearest Drug Change” = the closest medication adjustment — if blank, the shift happened without any recent drug change.

Two drug regimens might both average 1 seizure per day, but one has steady 1-per-day while the other has 0 for a week then 7 in one day. This tells them apart. CV (coefficient of variation) measures how unpredictable seizures are — lower is more stable. Dispersion shows clustering: > 1 means seizures come in bursts, ≈ 1 means random, < 1 means unusually regular. The scatter plot puts each regimen on a map: bottom-left (few seizures, low volatility) is the sweet spot. Red blocks on the timeline below mark burst episodes — clusters of unusually bad days.

When a drug is increased or decreased, the doctor does it in steps over days or weeks. This scores each ramp-up or ramp-down: was it done fast or slow? Did seizures get worse during the transition? Did things stabilize after? The step-line shows dosage changes over time, with daily seizures overlaid. Green bands = seizures improved after, red = worsened, yellow = unchanged.
